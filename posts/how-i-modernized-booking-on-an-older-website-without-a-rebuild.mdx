I built [Ocean Echo Adventures](https://oceanechoadventures.com/) back in 2019. Standard project — client needed a website, I built one, they got a site that explained their horse-riding business and made it feel real.

Five years later, they came back.

The site was still working fine. Still ranking. Still doing the job it was built to do. The problem wasn't the website — it was what happened after someone read it and decided they wanted to book. Clicking "Book now" opened an email draft. The booking process was just emailing the business and hoping the back-and-forth went smoothly.

When volume is low, that works. Once the business needs to track bookings, manage capacity, collect deposits, and send confirmations, it falls apart.

The obvious move at this point is a full rebuild. New stack, new design, clean slate. Completely justifiable.

I didn't do that.

## What I actually wanted to avoid

Rebuilding the site would have meant touching a lot of things that weren't broken.

SEO built up over five years. Content people were actually finding. A layout the client understood and knew how to work with. You rebuild all that and you're introducing risk across every layer — rankings, redirects, client retraining — to solve a problem that was really just about adding a transaction layer underneath the existing site.

So I treated the existing site as fixed and figured out what I could build around it.

## The stack I landed on

I wanted to use tools with free tiers where I could, because the business is small and baking in ongoing SaaS costs across five different services didn't make sense. Here's what I ended up with:

**[Cal.com](https://cal.com/)** for scheduling. Free tier, open source, and it has an API that lets you do things beyond what the UI exposes — which matters more than it sounds.

**[Convex](https://convex.dev/)** as the backend database. Real-time, serverless, free tier generous enough for this volume. It became the source of truth for booking state, payment records, slot capacity — everything the Cal.com webhooks feed into.

**[Resend](https://resend.com/)** for transactional email. Free tier covers the volume. Clean API.

**Fiserv** for payments. This one's not free — it's the payment gateway the business was already set up with, and it's not especially developer-friendly. But it's what they had, so I made it work.

The public Ocean Echo site stayed exactly as it was. What I built was a separate Next.js app — a booking engine — that the "Book now" button on the existing site now links into.

## Where it got more complicated than expected

The initial brief sounded simple: replace the email button with a real booking flow, add payment.

Then the actual requirements came out.

Ocean Echo is a horse-riding business. Not every person who tries to book should automatically get a payment link. Age and weight matter for the rides. Some bookings need a manual review before they're confirmed. The business wanted control over who moved forward.

So the flow became:

1. Someone requests a booking through the Cal.com embed
2. The Cal.com webhook fires, I store a pending approval in Convex
3. The team reviews it and either approves or rejects
4. On approval, the booking gets confirmed in Cal.com via API and the customer gets a payment link
5. Payment goes through Fiserv — fine, not great, but it works
6. Paid booking updates the Convex record and sends the confirmation email

That approval step changed the whole shape of the project. It's the difference between embedding a widget and building a state machine.

A few other things that ended up being real work:

**Capacity management.** The ride slots hold a maximum of 6 people. I track counts in Convex and when a timeslot fills up, I block the surrounding availability in Cal.com so nobody can squeeze in. For group bookings with multiple attendees, I use Cal.com's slot reservation API to hold consecutive windows so the whole group lands in the right place.

**Payment expiration.** If someone gets a payment link and doesn't pay within 6 hours, the booking gets cancelled, the Cal.com event is deleted, the reserved slots are released, and Convex marks the payment as expired. This runs on a cron job every 15 minutes. It sounds like a small detail until you forget to build it.

**Admin pricing controls.** Pricing lives in the database, not the code. Six ride types, each with their own price per person, a surcharge for private rides, a configurable deposit percentage, and an admin page the client can use to change any of it without touching a line of code. If the client has to email you every time the pricing changes, you haven't finished the job.

## What they ended up with

The 2019 site is still running, unchanged.

The booking engine sits next to it. Someone clicks "Book now," goes through Cal.com, gets reviewed by the team, pays a deposit through Fiserv, gets a confirmation email, and shows up. The team has a dashboard to approve or reject requests, see who's paid, adjust pricing, and manage capacity.

Running cost: Cal.com free tier, Convex free tier, Resend free tier, and Fiserv processing fees per transaction.

I don't know if this approach works every time. Sometimes a site does need to be rebuilt — the tech is unmaintainable, the design is actively hurting conversion, something structural is wrong. But that wasn't the case here. The site was fine. The business needed a booking engine.

So that's what I built.
